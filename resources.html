Implicit Bias Resources

The fields of technology and innovation are often heralded as beacons of progress, yet they are not immune to the systemic issues that pervade society. Two critical frameworks for understanding these issues are intersectionality and implicit bias. Implicit bias is a term that names the attitudes and stereotypes that can unconsciously shape one's thoughts, actions, and decisions without even realizing it. Intersectionality, coined by Kimberlé Crenshaw, examines how various social identities—such as race, gender, and socioeconomic status—intersect to create unique experiences of oppression or privilege. Together, these concepts illuminate how systemic inequalities manifest within the tech industry.

The tech industry is predominantly shaped by a homogenous group of individuals, often reflecting the privilege of white, male, and affluent backgrounds. This lack of diversity has significant implications for product design, workforce culture, and market reach. For instance, when predominantly white and male teams develop technologies, they may overlook the needs and experiences of underrepresented groups. This is evident in numerous case studies, such as facial recognition technology that exhibits bias against people of color, largely because the algorithms were trained on non-diverse datasets.

Intersectionality offers a lens to critique this homogeneity. Women of color in tech face compounded discrimination, often experiencing both sexism and racism. This dual burden can lead to their voices being marginalized in decision-making processes, exacerbating issues of representation and equity. For example, women of color are often underrepresented in leadership roles, resulting in a lack of diverse perspectives in product development. Companies that fail to adopt an intersectional approach may inadvertently create products that cater to a narrow demographic, limiting innovation and alienating large segments of the market.

Implicit bias plays a critical role in hiring practices within the tech industry. Research shows that resumes with "ethnic-sounding" names are often rated lower than those with "white-sounding" names, even when qualifications are identical. This bias can extend to the evaluation of candidates during interviews and performance reviews. For instance, women may face scrutiny that their male counterparts do not, leading to disparities in promotions and job retention.

Furthermore, implicit bias contributes to a workplace culture that may unwittingly exclude certain groups. This can manifest in microaggressions, which are subtle, often unintentional comments or actions that can marginalize individuals based on their identity. The cumulative effect of these biases creates an environment where diverse voices feel undervalued or silenced, further perpetuating the cycle of exclusion.

To do your part of helping create a more equitable world, please take the time to learn more about implicit bias by exploring the following resources:

  1. Implicit Bias Test

    Harvard University's Implicit Association Test (IAT), lead by Project Implicit, is a key assessment tool for understanding one's unconscious biases.

  2. National Equity Project's Framework on Implicit Bias and Structural Racialization

    This article written by Kathleen Osta and Hugh Vasquez goes into depth about why discussing structural racism is crucial when unpacking implicit bias.

  3. UCLA Office of Equity, Diversity, and Inclusion: Implicit Bias Video Series

    This free series from the University of California, Los Angeles features six videos discussing implicit biases and their affects.

  4. National Research Mentoring Network (NRMN) Unconscious Bias Course

    The NRMN provides professional-level mentorship and programming with a focus on diversity, equity, and inclusion. The Unconsious Bias Course they offer is a multi-module program that awards certifications upon completion of each module.

  5. National Association of Independent Schools (NAIS), Kimberlé Crenshaw: What is Intersectionality?

    Kimberlé Crenshaw, a 2017 NAIS People of Color Conference speaker, civil rights advocate, and professor at UCLA School of Law and Columbia Law School, talks about intersectional theory, the study of how overlapping or intersecting social identities—and particularly minority identities—relate to systems and structures of discrimination.
